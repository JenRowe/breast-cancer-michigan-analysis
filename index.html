Final recommendation

Mortality disparities are driven by post-screening factors, not screening access

Across 78 Michigan counties, mammography screening rates are consistently high — yet mortality varies widely. The evidence points clearly to what happens after a positive screening: delayed follow-up, poor care coordination, geographic barriers to treatment, and socioeconomic conditions that increase baseline vulnerability. Expanding screening alone will not close this gap. Intervention must focus on the care pathway beyond detection.

Screening explains near-zero variation in mortality (r ≈ 0)
Age, poverty & food insecurity are the strongest correlates
5 priority counties account for ~40% of all deaths
Focus: follow-up care, referral pathways, geographic access
Key takeaways
Screening alone doesn't explain mortality
Breast cancer mortality varies widely across Michigan counties, but is not explained by screening rates alone. Outcomes are more strongly associated with demographic and socioeconomic factors, and with differences in access to follow-up and treatment after detection.
Two distinct patterns of need
Urban counties — Wayne, Oakland, and Macomb — contribute the greatest overall burden due to population size. Smaller rural counties — Luce and Lake — experience disproportionately high mortality rates. Both patterns are real and require different responses.
40% of deaths in five counties
The top five priority counties account for approximately 40% of total breast cancer deaths in Michigan. This concentration means targeted intervention in these areas can have outsized statewide impact.
Move beyond screening access
Addressing mortality requires focusing on what happens after detection — care coordination, timeliness of follow-up, and access to treatment services. These post-screening factors are the actual drivers of the disparities observed across both high-burden and high-rate counties.
One metric misses half the problem
Focusing on mortality rate alone would overlook high-burden urban counties. Focusing on total deaths alone would overlook high-rate rural counties. Only a combined approach — as used in this analysis — captures the full picture of need across Michigan.

04 — Access to healthcare

Uninsurance and geography compound post-screening barriers

Screening only helps when patients can access what comes next. Uninsurance, distance to treatment centers, and lack of care coordination prevent abnormal results from translating into timely care — and these barriers concentrate in the same high-mortality counties.

06 — Implications and next steps

From pattern to action

Reducing mortality requires moving past screening access and focusing on what happens after detection. These steps address the actual drivers of disparity.

  • Measure follow-up after abnormal screening. Track whether patients complete diagnostic imaging or biopsy within 90 days. Health systems in Wayne, Oakland, and Macomb can use existing Medicaid claims data to identify care coordination gaps without new infrastructure.
  • Strengthen referral pathways to regional cancer centers. Standardize referral workflows from local providers to Detroit, Ann Arbor, and Grand Rapids hubs. Prioritize faster scheduling for patients in high-mortality counties. The bottleneck is not finding cancer — it is getting patients to treatment efficiently.
  • Map geographic access to diagnostic and treatment services. Quantify travel time to the nearest cancer facility for Luce and Lake counties. Geographic isolation is the most likely explanation for their mortality rates being the highest in the state while screening rates are comparable to lower-mortality counties.
  • Target socioeconomic interventions in high-poverty counties. Wayne (20.2% poverty) and Lake (20.3% poverty) face compounding disadvantage across poverty, food insecurity, and unemployment simultaneously. These factors amplify mortality risk beyond what any screening program alone can offset.
  • Monitor outcomes over time using measurable indicators. Use mortality rate, stage at diagnosis, and time to diagnosis as primary tracking metrics — not screening rates — to evaluate whether targeted interventions are reducing disparities across the five priority counties over time.
